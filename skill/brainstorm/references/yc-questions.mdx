# YC Product Diagnostic Questions

The six forcing questions for startup mode brainstorming. Ask **ONE AT A TIME**. Push until the answer is specific and uncomfortable.

## Operating Principles

**Specificity is the only currency.** Vague answers get pushed until specific.

**Interest is not demand.** Waitlists, signups, "that's interesting" — none of it counts. Only behavior and money count.

**The user's words beat the founder's pitch.** The gap between marketing copy and what users say is the value — that's the truth.

---

## Q1: Demand Reality

> "What's the strongest evidence that someone actually wants this — not 'is interested,' but would be genuinely upset if it disappeared tomorrow?"

### What This Reveals

- Validates real demand vs. hypothetical interest
- Identifies users with emotional investment in the solution
- Distinguishes between "nice to have" and "can't live without"

### Red Flags

- "People said they'd use it" — not evidence
- "We have waitlist signups" — still not behavior
- "Competitors exist" — doesn't prove your solution matters

### Green Flags

- Users actively complaining when service is down
- People paying money (even small amounts)
- Organic word-of-mouth referrals

---

## Q2: Status Quo

> "What are your users doing right now to solve this problem? What does that workaround cost them?"

### What This Reveals

- The actual competition is often "nothing" or "spreadsheets"
- Quantifies pain in time, money, or frustration
- Identifies switching costs and inertia

### Red Flags

- "There's no solution" — usually means no painful problem
- Users aren't looking for alternatives

### Green Flags

- Users have cobbled together multiple tools
- Clear time/money cost you can undercut
- Workarounds that are obviously painful

---

## Q3: Desperate Specificity

> "Name the actual human who needs this most. What's their title? What gets them fired?"

### What This Reveals

- Forces identification of a specific buyer/user
- Connects product to real career/business outcomes
- Identifies decision-makers and stakeholders

### Red Flags

- "Small businesses" — too broad
- "Developers" — still too broad
- No clear job title or role

### Green Flags

- "VP of Sales at B2B SaaS companies with 50-200 employees"
- Clear KPI they're measured on
- Personal career risk if problem isn't solved

---

## Q4: Narrowest Wedge

> "What's the smallest possible version someone would pay real money for — this week?"

### What This Reveals

- Identifies MVP scope
- Forces prioritization of core value
- Tests pricing hypothesis

### Red Flags

- "The full platform" — thinking too big
- "Free tier first" — avoiding pricing validation

### Green Flags

- Single feature that solves immediate pain
- Clear path to first dollar
- Can be built in days, not months

---

## Q5: Observation & Surprise

> "Have you watched someone use this without helping them? What surprised you?"

### What This Reveals

- Gap between expected and actual user behavior
- UX problems you can't see from inside
- Features users ignore vs. value

### Red Flags

- "We haven't launched yet" — get something in front of users
- "Our team tested it" — not real users
- Nothing surprised you — probably not watching closely

### Green Flags

- Users use features in unexpected ways
- Users struggle with things you thought were obvious
- Users ignore features you worked hard on

---

## Q6: Future-Fit

> "If the world looks different in 3 years, does your product become more essential or less?"

### What This Reveals

- Alignment with macro trends
- Long-term defensibility
- Risk of obsolescence

### Red Flags

- Product only makes sense in current environment
- Dependent on platform/app store policies
- Technology being replaced

### Green Flags

- Trends (remote work, AI adoption, regulatory changes) make problem worse
- Problem grows with market trends
- Building on growing platforms/infrastructure
